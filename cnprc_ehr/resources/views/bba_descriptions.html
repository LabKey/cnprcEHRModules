Introduction to BBA project

The BioBehavioral Assessment (BBA) program was developed in 2001 to assess infant rhesus monkeys’ temperament, emotionality, and behavioral and physiological reactivity. This information can be used by Primate Center staff and by Center research scientists to test ideas about how differences among individuals in these processes could affect management practices (e.g., what are the characteristics of animals that develop abnormal behaviors, or poor maternal behavior, or poor adaptation during pairings) or research outcomes (e.g., what are the characteristics of animals that progress to disease most rapidly, or that adjust well to chairing, or that are outliers in my data set; how should I assign animals to experimental groups?).

The rationale for this project is the result of more than two decades of research in Psychology, Biological Psychology, and Primatology. This research has shown that many biobehavioral traits that animals possess (often described as temperament, personality, or reactivity) are present and measurable in early life, and are stable throughout life. Individual differences in these traits have been linked to variation in important outcomes, ranging from social behavior, to alcohol consumption, to brain function, to disease processes.

John Capitanio is Principal Investigator on the project, which also originally included Bill Mason and Sally Mendoza as collaborating investigators. The research was initially funded as a Resource Research project on the CNPRC base grant (RR000169), and then as an R24 grant from NCRR/ORIP. The grant was re-funded in 2009, and again in 2014. The grant ID is R24 OD010962.

General Protocol

Infant monkeys (from the field cages, corncribs, indoor breeding colony, or nursery) are tested for a single 25 hour period when they are between the ages of 90 and 120 days in cohorts of 5-8 animals each. For a given cohort, infants and their mothers (or peers, in the case of nursery-reared animals) are separated from each other. Mothers from the field cages and corncribs are transported to a holding area (typically the THPAD or CC301), and infants are taken to a testing/housing area. Infants arrive by 0900 hrs. and are housed individually with ad lib access to food, a novel object, and a stuffed toy duck and towel in their cages. Infants are returned to their mothers/peers at 1000 hrs. on Day 2. During the 25-hr period, animals are assessed behaviorally and physiologically in a number of standardized situations to identify individual differences in biobehavioral organization:

Living Cage Observations. To assess their responses to separation and relocation, we observe each infant for a 5-min period at the beginning and at the end of the 25 hr. separation period. For these assessments, an observer sits in front of the rack of cages at a distance of 8 feet. Observers are instructed to minimize all direct eye contact with the animals during data collection. Each animal is observed individually (focal animal sampling), and data are recorded with the use of the Observer program (Noldus, 1991). Principal measures are activity, vocalization, and contact with objects.

Preferential Look Test. This test is designed to assess the infants' ability to recognized the difference between a stimulus it has just seen and a new stimulus (ie, recognition memory), and we use procedures adapted from Gunderson et al. (1987). Each infant is carried to a small test cage that is placed in front of a large video monitor and receives 10 trials. On each trial the animal is shown two identical stimuli (objects or pictures), followed by a brief interval, after which the familiar object and a novel object are displayed. The principal measure of interest is duration of looking at the novel object. This test reflects visual recognition memory, and performance has been related to later cognitive impairment and developmental problems.

Video Playback. The purpose of this test is to assess the infants' responsiveness to a social stimulus presented via color videotape. The 10-min stimulus tape consists of three sequences of an unfamiliar adult male rhesus macaque displaying viewer-directed aggressive behavior, interspersed with four segments of the same male displaying nonsocial (i.e., neutral) behavior. Each subject is carried to the test cage, which is situated in front of the video monitor. The infant's behaviors while it views the aggression and nonsocial sequences of the videotape are recorded and the infant is then returned to its living cage. Responses of interest include positional and social behavior directed at the video image, and have been related, in adult males, to major personality dimensions including Sociability (Capitanio, 2002).

Human Intruder Test. The goal of this test is to assess the infants' responsiveness under standardized conditions of the challenge of a relatively non-threatening human (presenting her face in profile) and a more threatening human (staring at the infant). Each condition is presented at two distances. The infant is carried to the test cage and experiences four 1-min trials. For the first trial, an unfamiliar human sits approximately 3 ft in front of the cage, presenting her left profile. After 1 min, the human, maintaining a profile orientation, moves to within 12 inches of the test cage. One minute later, the human returns to the more distant location and attempts to maintain direct eye contact with the infant for 1 minute, following which the human again moves to within 12 inches of the test cage and attempts to maintain eye contact for 1 min. These conditions are designated, respectively, profile far, profile near, stare far, stare near. Responses of interest include positional, activity, and emotional behaviors.

Blood Sampling. Blood is sampled on four occasions to assess infants' responses to separation and relocation, and to determine the regulatory characteristics of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Four blood samples are drawn via femoral venipuncture from each monkey and are used to assess concentrations of plasma cortisol. The first sample (1 ml) is taken following completion of the Living Cage observations on Day 1 (2 hrs. after arrival in the test room), and is also used for a complete blood count and to assess numbers of CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells using flow cytometry. After completion of the Human Intruder testing (at 1600 hrs.), a second sample (0.5 ml) is taken, after which each subject is given 500 µg/kg dexamethasone i.m. The third sample (0.5 ml) is taken following the living cage observations on Day 2 (at 0830 hrs.), and should reflect cortisol concentrations that are suppressed by the dexamethasone. Failure to suppress cortisol in humans is associated with negative affective characteristics (including depression). Immediately after Sample 3, each subject is injected with 2.5 IU ACTH i.m., and a fourth sample (0.5 ml) is taken 30 min later. The dexamethasone and ACTH stimulation tests are clinical tests used routinely to determine the integrity of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal system. We have data suggesting that the response of the HPA system is traitlike – individual differences in cortisol concentrations (including responses to dexamethasone suppression) are maintained over time (Capitanio, Mendoza, Lerche, 1998).

Novel Objects. The goal of this test is to assess the infants' responsiveness by measuring their activity with unfamiliar objects in their living cages. The first object (black hollow cylinder with ridges on the exterior surface, 9 cm long x 4 cm in diameter, weighing 0.090 kg) is present when the animal is first placed in the living cage, and remains in the cage until after the second blood sample is drawn. The second object (white, crayon-shaped object with ridges on the outer surface, same dimensions and weight) is then placed in the cage and remains until the assessments ends on Day 2. Each object contains an Actiwatch activity monitor that records the force exerted on the object.

Temperament Ratings. The goal of the rating is to provide an overall "thumbnail" picture of each infant's behavioral characteristics during the testing period. At the end of the 25 hr. assessment period, just before the infant is returned to its mother/cage-mates and housing situation, it is rated by the technician who performed the assessments. The rating instrument consists of 20 adjectives (e.g., calm, bold, excitable, playful, tense) and their definitions. The technician rates each animal on each adjective using a seven-point scale, reflecting "total absence" to "extremely large amount" of the characteristic displayed. From these ratings, major dimensions of temperament can be obtained (e.g., Capitanio, 1999).

Following the temperament rating, mother-reared infants from the field cages and corncribs are reunited with their mothers in the mothers’ holding cages. Mothers and infants are observed for approximately 1 hr. to insure that the mothers accept the infants, and to allow an opportunity for the infants to suckle. Mother-infant pairs are then returned to their original living cages. Informal observations continue to insure that the re-introductions to the cages occur safely. Nursery-reared animals are immediately returned to the nursery, and infants from the indoor breeding colony are returned directly to their mothers in their living cages.

Rearing Condition

Animals are classified into one of four rearing conditions based on where they were reared during their first 3-4 months of life. Below are brief descriptions of each rearing condition. Note that specific details for any given animal may vary.

NC - animals reared in the field cages. Field cages contain animals of multiple ages and sexes, with up to 150 animals in each cage.

CC - animals reared in the corncribs. Corncribs typically contain one adult male, 3-6 adult females, and any previous offspring.

MR - animals are born into the indoor breeding colony and are "mother-reared". Housing involves a single mother-infant pair living in a standard-sized individual cage. A given mother-infant pair may have direct contact with another mother-infant pair in an adjacent cage for several hours per day. Note that this is a heterogeneous category, in that in some cases, infants were conceived in the field cages, but due to events (eg, a cage war) were relocated indoors during gestation. See the following paper to learn how this can make a difference: Elfenbein, H.A., Del Rosso, L., McCowan, B., Capitanio, J.P. Effect of indoor compared with outdoor location during gestation on the incidence of diarrhea in indoor-reared rhesus macaques (Macaca mulatta). Journal of AALAS, 2016, 55: 277-290.

NR - animals are raised in the nursery. NR infants are typically derived from the field cages by separating them from their mothers on the day of birth and placing them in incubators (each containing a stuffed animal toy and towels to cling to) for approximately 30 days, after which they are moved into individual units of a quad cage. After 1 or 2 days of habituation, the barriers between adjacent units are removed so that infants can interact in pairs. In some years, infants were formed into pairs for 6 hours per day, while in other years barriers were removed permanently, resulting in continuous, dyadic peer-housing.

Weight at Testing

Upon arrival in the testing area, animals are weighed using a standard CNPRC Fairbanks scale. This weight is the value we use to determine doses of dexamethasone and ACTH.

SPF Status

This code indicates whether (Y) or not (N) an animal is an SPF animal. Note that some SPF animals are listed as nursery reared, and some are listed as being born into an SPF field cage. This code does NOT distinguish between the different levels of SPF that we have at CNPRC.

Behavioral Responsiveness

These data are taken from the "Living Cage Observations," which are five minute samples of behavior recorded live on two occasions during the 25-hr. assessment period. On Day 1, the observations begin about 15 minutes after the animals are relocated to the test room (0915), and on Day 2, they begin at 0700. Exploratory and Confirmatory factor analyses were performed on the behavioral data and are described in some detail in the following publication:

Golub, M.S., Hogrefe, C.E., Widaman, K.F., Capitanio, J.P. Iron deficiency anemia and affective response in rhesus monkey infants. Developmental Psychobiology, 2009, 51, 47-59.

These analyses identified two behavioral composites (Activity and Emotionality) for Day 1 data. Identical composites were created for the Day 2 data. Note that the Day 1 composites reflect how animals responded immediately upon separation from mother/peer and relocation to a novel environment. The data from Day 2 reflect adaptation to the situation. In very general terms, animals (especially those from the field cages) often showed low Activity during the Day 1 observations. Some animals continued to display low Activity on Day 2, but others seemed to behave much more normally. Thus, we believe Day 2 data reflect the extent to which animals became more comfortable in the situation.

    ACTIVITY comprises the following behaviors
  • proportion of total time spent locomoting;
  • proportion of total time spent NOT hanging from top/side of cage;
  • rate of environmental exploration;
  • dichotomous variable for whether animal ate food (+) or not;
  • dichotomous variable for whether animal drank water (+) or not;
  • dichotomous variable for whether animal crouched (+) or not.
    EMOTIONALITY comprises the following behaviors
  • rate of cooing;
  • rate of barking;
  • dichotomous variable of whether the animal scratched (+) or not;
  • dichotomous variable of whether the animal displayed threats (+) or not;
  • dichotomous variable of whether the animal lipsmacked (+) or not.

Temperament Ratings

At the end of the 25-hr. testing period, the technician rated each animal on listing of 20 trait adjectives, with a goal of obtaining overall ‘thumbnail’ assessments of the animals’ behavioral characteristics during the period of testing. We believe these trait ratings reflect stable, individual difference characteristics of the animals.

Scores on these traits were subjected to exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis, described in the following paper:

Golub, M.S., Hogrefe, C.E., Widaman, K.F., Capitanio, J.P. Iron deficiency anemia and affective response in rhesus monkey infants. Developmental Psychobiology, 2009, 51, 47-59.

    Four factors were found. Once each scale was constructed (by adding the values of the individual items), the scale was then z-scored. The scales were named based on the factor analysis.
  • Vigilant: vigilant, NOT depressed, NOT tense, NOT timid.
  • Gentle: gentle, calm, flexible, curious.
  • Confident: confident, bold, active, curious, playful.
  • Nervous: nervous, fearful, timid, NOT calm, NOT confident.

Human Intruder

Many different research laboratories conduct some type of Human Intruder (HI) test to assess a variety of constructs, like aggression, behavioral inhibition, etc. For the BBA program, we use a brief version comprising four 1-minute trials, with the technician presenting her profile face from a far (~1 meter) distance, profile from a near (~0.3 meter) distance, full frontal face from the far, and full frontal face from the near, distances. There are many measures one can take from this test, but we performed exploratory and confirmatory factor analyses to identify the factors that underlie the behaviors seen. When we included behaviors recorded from the four separate conditions, the same behaviors tended to be found on the same factors, so we simply combined data across the four trials and re-ran the analyses. You can find the details of the analysis here:

Gottlieb, D.H., Capitanio, J.P. Latent variables affecting behavioral response to the human intruder test in infant rhesus monkeys (Macaca mulatta). American Journal of Primatology, 2013, 75, 314-323.

    The following four factors were found:
  • HIActivity: active, cageshake, environment explore.
  • HIEmotionality: convulsive jerk, fear grimace, self-clasp, vocal coo.
  • HIAggression: threat, vocal bark, vocal other.
  • HIDisplacement: tooth grind, yawn.

Behavioral Inhibition Index

Behavioral inhibition (BI) is a major temperament trait for many species of mammals, including humans. BI is typically assessed in infancy/toddlerhood in humans, and is considered an important risk factor for later anxiety (especially social anxiety) and depression in people. For the BBA program, we define BI as having scores below zero for four measures: Day 1 Activity, Day 1 Emotionality, Day 2 Activity, and Day 2 Emotionality. This index of BI has been linked to later behavior, to genetic differences, to metabolomic differences, and to differences in gene expression in the brain.

This index is displayed as either a 0, indicating the animals’ responses did not meet the criteria for BI, or 1, indicating the animals’ responses did meet the criteria.

Z-Score

Most of the data presented are in z-score format. Z-scores (sometimes called “standard scores”) are derived by taking an individual’s actual score, subtracting the mean of the sample, then dividing by the standard deviation (which is a measure of variability). The z-score tells you how extreme from the “average” an individual’s score is. In z-score units, the average score is zero. If the measure is normally distributed, here’s how z-scores translate into where that given animal is in the broader sample:

If the z-score is:       then the animals' score is higher than this % of the sample:
-1.65 5%
-1.00 16%
0 50%
1.00 84%
1.65 95%

If you want more precise values, use this calculator:
http://davidmlane.com/hyperstat/z_table.html

Note that for each data set, z-scores were calculated independently for each year of testing. Thus, all values are relative to each other for all animals within a single test year, like 2003. If you were to compare two animals, one from 2003 and one from 2005, that each had a score of 1.00 for a given measure, you would know that they each had higher scores than 84% of their respective yearly testing cohorts. You should NOT conclude that they had the exact same ACTUAL score value, since values from year to year can vary slightly, owing to a number of factors.

Plasma Cortisol

Cortisol is a glucocorticoid, a hormone released by the adrenal cortex, which causes release of glucose into the circulation. It is secreted in response to ACTH, which is a peptide released by the pituitary. Cortisol is often thought of as a “stress” hormone. It is certainly released in response to situations that most would perceive as stressful, which makes some sense – providing glucose can help you recover from excessive emotional and physical exertion. However, cortisol concentrations reflect lots of different things. For example, cortisol concentrations are generally higher in the morning than in the afternoon in most people (again, this makes sense, in that it provides the energy for your day), but not in all people – about half the people who suffer from clinical depression do not show this circadian rhythm of cortisol, for example.

In the BBA project, we assessed cortisol at four time points.

Sample 1 was drawn at 1100 hours on Day 1, which is about two hours after the infants had been separated from their mothers and relocated to the testing environment. Consequently, these values are likely “stressed” values.

Sample 2 was drawn at 1600 hours on Day 1, at the conclusion of the day’s behavioral testing. In the absence of stressful circumstances, we would expect Sample 1 values to be higher than Sample 2 values, because cortisol is naturally higher in the morning than in the afternoon. In the BBA animals, in contrast, most animals showed elevations at Sample 2, presumably reflecting continued stress associated with separation and relocation. This was often not true of nursery-raised animals, however (see Capitanio, J.P., Mendoza, S.P., Mason, W.A., Maninger, N. Rearing environment and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal regulation in young rhesus monkeys (Macaca mulatta). Developmental Psychobiology, 2005, 46, 318-330).

Sample 3 was drawn at 0830 hours on Day 2, after the Day 2 Living Cage Observations had been performed. Immediately after Sample 2 was drawn on an animal, it received an injection of dexamethasone (500 ug/kg) i.m., which is a potent synthetic glucocorticoid. The “dexamethasone suppression test” is a standard clinical test of negative feedback regulation in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal system, and dex typically suppresses cortisol output by the adrenal cortex when measured several hours later. Sample 3 cortisol levels should reflect at least some suppression by dexamethasone.

Sample 4 was drawn at 0900 hours on Day 2. Immediately after Sample 3 was drawn, each animal was injected with 2.5 IU of ACTH. Since ACTH normally stimulates cortisol secretion from the adrenal cortex, we would expect that cortisol concentrations would rise, which they did for nearly all animals.

Over the years, cortisol has been assayed using two different platforms by the Endocrine Core at CNPRC. Initially, radioimmunoassay was used, but when this kit was discontinued, we moved to the Centaur platform currently in use by that Core. Nancy Gee in the Endocrine Core, or John Capitanio, can provide additional information on the assay from the newer platform.

Note that the values presented here are z-scores, not the actual cortisol concentrations. This is in keeping with the goal of helping users determine whether the animals of interest to them responded in an unusual fashion relative to the other animals in the sample. Thus, while a z-score of “0" reflects the mean value for each of the four cortisol samples, the actual cortisol concentration corresponding to that mean value differs by sample. If you are interested in the actual cortisol values, contact John Capitanio.

Serotonin transporter promoter genotype

Serotonin (also known as 5-hydroxytryptamine, or 5-HT) is a monoamine neurotransmitter found in the brain. Serotonin is also found in great quantities in the gut, and it is a molecule that has effects on the immune system. In the brain, serotonin is associated with mood and emotion, and is a target of therapeutic intervention for individuals suffering from mood disorders (e.g., SSRIs, or selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, are commonly prescribed antidepressants).

Once a serotonin neuron "fires," serotonin is released into the synaptic cleft, where it can have its effect on the downstream neuron. Serotonin is then taken back into the pre-synaptic cell (i.e., the cell that released it in the first place) in a process called reuptake, where the serotonin can be repackaged and recycled. Reuptake relies on the presence of a protein called a transporter (the serotonin transporter, or 5-HTT) in the membrane of the presynaptic cell. In humans and in rhesus monkeys, the gene that codes for this protein has a polymorphism in the promoter region of the gene (promoters are regions of gene that determine whether and how the gene will be transcribed). This region is often referred to as the serotonin transporter gene-linked polymorphic region, or 5-HTTLPR, which is sometimes referred to as SLC6A4.

For the most part, there are two forms of the gene, a "short" allele and a "long" allele (there is some evidence for other, more rare alleles, which we ignore for now). Thus, an individual may have three genotypes (since there is one form of the gene on each of the pair of chromosomes - chromosome pair 17 in humans, and pair 16 in rhesus): short/short, short/long, and long/long. The short allele seems to result in reduced efficiency of the transcription process. Several studies, in both humans and rhesus, have shown that possession of one or two short alleles (whether only one is needed or two are needed depends on the specific study) is associated with behavioral outcomes that are often described as anxious, fearful, or aggressive in nature, and with physiological outcomes, such as regulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal response. In many cases, these outcomes are particularly evident when individuals with a short allele also have had some form of early adverse experience, such as, for rhesus monkeys, nursery-rearing. You can read more about these outcomes in: Rogers J, Kaplan J, Garcia R, Shelledy W, Nair S, Cameron J. Mapping of the serotonin transporter locus (SLC6A4) to rhesus chromosome 16 using genetic linkage. Cytogenetic and Genome Research 112:341a, 2006.

Because our dataset is so large, we have also identified many cases of a rare allele for this gene, a so-called "extra-long", which is designated as XL. Animals can have any combination of genes with this allele as well: S/XL, L/XL, or XL/XL. We note that there are some rare animals with three genes, specifically S/L/XL. All of these will be identifiable in the database.

    Here we present the genotype information for BBA-assessed animals. Overall in our colony, based on about 4100 animals that have been genotyped for this polymorphism, we find:
  • 58% have the l/l genotype
  • 35% have the s/l genotype
  • 6% have the s/s genotype
  • <2% with the rare genotypes.

Monoamine oxidase A genotype

Monoamine oxidase A is an enzyme whose gene is on the X chromosome. It is responsible for the breakdown of various neurotransmitters, such as norepinephrine, epinephrine, serotonin, and dopamine. These neurotransmitters are found throughout the brain, and are involved with emotion, among other things.

In humans and in rhesus monkeys, there is a promoter region that is polymorphic. Specifically, there is variation in the length of a repetitive sequence; this is referred to as a variable number of tandem repeats, or VNTR. While the number of repeats differs between humans and rhesus, there appears to be overlap in outcome. In rhesus monkeys, the main number of repeats are labelled 5, 6, and 7 repeats (there may be a small number of other repeats as well). A paper by Newman et al. (Newman TK, Syagailo YV, Barr CS, Wendland JR, Champoux M, Graessle M, Suomi SJ, Higley JD, Lesch KP. Monoamine oxidase A gene promoter variation and rearing experience influences aggressive behavior in rhesus monkeys. Biol Psychiatry 57:167-172, 2005) reported that the 7-repeat allele results in lower transcriptional activity than do the 5- and 6-repeats. The outcomes associated with variation in this gene have been related to aggressive and antisocial behavior in humans, particularly if the individuals have had adverse early life experiences. In rhesus monkeys, we have reported genotype by rearing interactions (Karere GM, Kinnally EL, Sanchez JN, Famula TR, Lyons LA, Capitanio JP. What is an "adverse" environment? Interactions of rearing experiences and MAOA genotype in rhesus monkeys. Biol Psychiatry 65:770-777, 2009): No effects of genotype were found for infants reared by mothers in our field cages. In contrast, animals reared in corncribs were more likely to display aggression, which was especially true of animals possessing the low-activity MAOA genotype. In addition, animals with low-activity genotypes that had experienced restricted mother rearing (in our time-mate colony) showed more anxious behavior (scratch, yawn).

Because MAO-A is on the X-chromosome, genotypes are a bit more complicated than for the serotonin transporter. Males have only one X chromosome, so we have 5/-, 6/-, or 7/- genotypes (males are thus referred to as hemizygous, possessing only one form of the gene). Because females have two X chromosomes, they can have 5/5, 5/6, 5/7, 6/6, 6/7, or 7/7 genotypes. We don’t know a lot about how the heterozygous females (eg, the 5/6, 5/7, or 6/7 genotypes) behave genetically, because we don’t know which X-chromosome is inactivated in any given cell. Although since both the 5- and 6-repeat alleles are considered high activity, the 5/6 animals may not be that different from 5/5 or 6/6 animals. Based up on about 4200 genotypes from CNPRC animals we find the following genotype frequencies:

  • 5/5 females and 5/- males (all high activity) are 19.4% of the population
  • 6/6 females and 6/- males (all high activity) are 19.3% of the population
  • 7/7 females and 7/- males (all low activity) are 28.0% of the population
  • 5/6 females (presumably all high activity) are 9.4% of the population
  • 5/7 females (presumably mixed activity) are 12.2% of the population
  • 6/7 females (presumably mixed activity) are 11.1% of the population
  • <1% with rare genotypes

We note that we have also found other, rare variants of this gene as well, including a "4" allele, and a "6.5" allele. Thus there can be other combinations of genotypes involving these rare alleles.
